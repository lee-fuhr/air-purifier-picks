Frequently asked questions

What filter type is best for allergies?

True HEPA is the standard for allergy filtration, it captures 99.97% of particles 0.3 microns and larger, which includes pollen (10-100 microns), dust mite particles (1-10 microns), and most pet dander. Activated carbon handles odors but doesn't filter allergen particles.

Does an air purifier help with seasonal allergies?

Significantly indoors. Air purifiers don't stop outdoor exposure, but they reduce the indoor particle load that builds up when pollen enters through doors, windows, and clothing. Running a purifier in the bedroom improves the 8 hours of sleep exposure meaningfully.

Do air purifiers help with dust mites?

They capture airborne dust mite particles and fecal matter. They don't penetrate mattresses, upholstery, or carpeting where dust mites live. Combined with allergen-barrier mattress covers and regular washing, air purifiers reduce the indoor dust mite burden significantly.

What CADR do I need for allergy relief?

Match CADR to room size: a CADR of ~200 for a 300 sq ft bedroom, ~350 for a larger living room. The ACH (air changes per hour) concept: allergy sufferers benefit from 4-5 complete air changes per hour. Calculate: CADR × 60 ÷ room volume (cubic feet) = ACH. Aim for 4+.

Where should I place an air purifier for allergies?

In the room where you spend the most time, typically the bedroom. Place it away from walls and corners for maximum airflow circulation. Running it continuously in the bedroom ensures the air you breathe during sleep is filtered, which matters most for allergy symptoms on waking.
