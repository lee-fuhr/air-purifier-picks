Air quality is one of those things you don’t think about until you do. Whether you’re dealing with seasonal allergies, pet dander, urban smog, or just the dust that seems to appear from nowhere, a good air purifier can make a real difference in how you feel every day. The trick is finding one that fits your room size, your noise tolerance, and your budget without being a pain to maintain. Most purifiers work the same way: a fan pulls air in, passes it through a filter (usually a HEPA or a hybrid system), and pushes clean air back out. The key specs to pay attention to are the CADR (clean air delivery rate) for your room size, the type of filter it uses, and the long-term cost of replacement filters. You don’t need to obsess over every number, but understanding these basics will help you avoid buying something that’s either too weak or way more than you need.

Frequently asked questions

What is CADR and why does it matter?

CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate) measures how quickly a purifier removes smoke, dust, and pollen from a specific room volume, measured in cubic feet per minute. It's the only standardized, independent performance rating for air purifiers. A CADR of 200+ smoke is strong for a bedroom; 300+ covers larger spaces. Room size claims from manufacturers are often optimistic, CADR is the honest metric.

Do I need True HEPA or will regular HEPA work?

True HEPA filters 99.97% of particles 0.3 microns or larger, which includes dust, pollen, mold spores, and most pet dander. Regular HEPA has no standardized definition and may filter less effectively. For allergy and asthma sufferers, True HEPA is the minimum. The Coway, Levoit Core series, and Winix all use True HEPA.

How often do air purifier filters need replacing?

Pre-filters: wash every 2-4 weeks. True HEPA filters: replace every 6-12 months depending on air quality. Activated carbon filters: 3-6 months in high-odor environments. Most purifiers have filter replacement indicators. Budget $40-80/year for filters on most quality machines.

Is it safe to run an air purifier 24/7?

Yes, quality air purifiers are designed for continuous operation. Running 24/7 on auto mode maintains air quality consistently rather than recovering periodically. Eco modes (like Coway's) automatically reduce fan speed when air quality is good, lowering noise and energy use.

Do ionizers in air purifiers help or hurt?

Ionizers charge particles to make them stick to surfaces or each other, aiding filtration. However, some ionizers produce trace ozone as a byproduct, which can irritate lungs, particularly for asthma sufferers. True HEPA filtration without ionization is the safe default; if an ionizer is present, look for an off switch.
