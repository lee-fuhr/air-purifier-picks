HEPA vs True HEPA air filters

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

“True HEPA” is a marketing term, not an official standard, but it usually means the filter meets the formal HEPA requirement of capturing at least 99.97% of particles 0.3 microns in size. A filter labeled simply “HEPA” or “HEPA-type” may or may not meet that standard, so when buying an air purifier, look for a “True HEPA” claim from a reputable brand or, better yet, an actual certified test result.

What does “HEPA” actually stand for?

HEPA stands for High Efficiency Particulate Air, and it refers to a specific standard, not a brand or type of material. The original U.S. Department of Energy definition requires a HEPA filter to capture at least 99.97% of airborne particles that are 0.3 microns in diameter. That 0.3 micron size is the hardest to catch, particles both larger and smaller are actually easier to trap, so it’s the benchmark for true performance. Filters that meet this standard are used in hospitals, clean rooms, and anywhere that extremely low particulate levels are required. But in the consumer market, the term “HEPA” has been used loosely, leading to a confusing mix of labels.

What is “True HEPA” and how is it different from just “HEPA”?

“True HEPA” is a term invented by manufacturers and retailers to distinguish filters that genuinely meet the 99.97% standard from those that don’t. In theory, a filter labeled “True HEPA” should perform exactly like a certified HEPA filter. But here’s the catch: unlike in commercial settings, there is no legal requirement in most countries for home air purifiers to undergo third-party testing to earn that “True” label. So the difference is mostly about marketing honesty. A filter marked simply “HEPA” might be a cheaper knockoff that only catches 90% or 95% at 0.3 microns. “True HEPA” is an implicit promise that it reaches the full standard, but you’re still relying on the brand’s reputation. That’s why it’s smart to choose purifiers from companies that publish their test results or carry certification from organizations like the Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers (AHAM).

Can a filter labeled “HEPA-type” or “HEPA-like” capture as much?

Almost never. Terms like “HEPA-type,” “HEPA-style,” or “HEPA-like” are red flags. They’re used to suggest high performance without committing to the actual standard. In independent tests, these filters often capture only 80% to 95% of 0.3 micron particles, which leaves many more pollutants in the air. If you’re buying for allergies, asthma, or smoke, that difference matters. A true HEPA filter will remove far more fine particles over time, and the room will clean more quickly. Stick with filters that explicitly say “True HEPA” or better yet, “Certified HEPA” if you see that label.

Why does the 0.3 micron particle size matter?

The 0.3 micron size is the “most penetrating particle size” (MPPS) for HEPA filters. Particles smaller than 0.3 microns are caught by diffusion, they bounce around and get trapped. Particles larger are caught by interception and impaction, they physically hit the fibers. But particles right around 0.3 microns tend to slip through more easily, so that’s the hardest test. By requiring 99.97% capture at 0.3 microns, the standard ensures the filter is highly efficient across the full range of common airborne particles like dust mite debris, pollen, mold spores, pet dander, and fine smoke. A filter that can’t meet that benchmark will let more of these irritants recirculate in your home.

Should you buy a purifier labeled “True HEPA” or one with a certified HEPA?

If you can find a purifier with an actual third-party certification, such as one tested by AHAM and listed with a CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate). that’s the most reliable choice. But most well-known home purifiers use “True HEPA” as their standard claim, and many of them perform excellently. Brands like Coway, Blueair, Winix, and Levoit offer True HEPA filters that consistently test above 99.97% in independent reviews. What matters most is the overall system: look for a high CADR rating for the room size, a good pre-filter to extend HEPA life, and a reputable company with solid customer service. A “True HEPA” label alone isn’t enough if the purifier is underpowered or poorly designed.

What about HEPA H13, H14, or medical grade labels?

European and international standards classify HEPA into grades: H13 (99.95% at 0.3 microns), H14 (99.995%), and so on. Some home purifiers advertise H13 or H14, which is technically even more efficient than the U.S. standard. For a typical home, H13 is already overkill, it’s the grade used in hospital wards, but it’s nice to have. “Medical grade” is another vague term. In practice, almost any True HEPA filter (99.97%) is sufficient for home allergy and asthma relief. The ultra-high grades like H14 or ULPA (99.9995%) add very little real-world benefit in a bedroom or living room and often restrict airflow, making the purifier louder or less efficient. Unless you have extreme sensitivities, a standard True HEPA filter from a good brand will do the job.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a legal definition of “True HEPA”?

No. “True HEPA” is a marketing phrase, not a legal or regulatory term. No government agency enforces its use. That’s why you should rely on third-party certifications like AHAM Verifide or published test reports from the manufacturer.

Can I trust a filter labeled only “HEPA” without the “True” prefix?

Not without checking the fine print. Some generic “HEPA” filters capture only 90–95% of 0.3 micron particles. If the brand is well-known and clearly states the efficiency (e.g., “captures 99.97% of particles”), you’re fine. Otherwise, aim for a model that explicitly says “True HEPA” or carries a certification.

Does a “True HEPA” filter capture viruses?

Yes, in the sense that viruses are often attached to larger droplets or carried on dust particles. A True HEPA filter will capture those carriers. Free-floating virus particles are smaller than 0.3 microns, but HEPA’s efficiency for sub‑0.3 micron particles is still very high (often above 99.9%), so it helps, but should not be your only protection. For virus removal, combine filtration with ventilation and hygiene measures.

How often should I replace a “True HEPA” filter?

Most manufacturers recommend replacement every 12 to 18 months, but it depends on usage and air quality. A good rule: replace when the filter looks dirty or when the purifier’s airflow drops noticeably. Many purifiers have indicator lights. Using a washable pre-filter can extend the HEPA filter’s life significantly.

Do all air purifiers with “True HEPA” perform the same?

No. Performance depends on the purifier’s fan strength, air seal design, and overall airflow. Two “True HEPA” purifiers can have very different Clean Air Delivery Rates (CADR). Always check CADR for the particle size that matters to you (smoke, dust, pollen) and match it to your room size. A high-efficiency filter in a weak purifier won’t clean a large room fast enough.

What’s the difference between “True HEPA” and “HEPA-type”?

“HEPA-type” generally means the filter does not meet the 99.97% standard, it’s a cheaper alternative that may still be decent but is not as efficient. “True HEPA” is a claim that it does meet the standard. When in doubt, look for the specific percentage (99.97%) or a certification mark.

In shortFor most homes, a “True HEPA” air purifier from a reputable brand will deliver excellent filtration, just make sure to check the CADR rating and room-size recommendations. Avoid vague labels like “HEPA-type” and look for companies that back their claims with published test data or third-party certification. That way you’ll get clean air without paying for marketing fluff.
